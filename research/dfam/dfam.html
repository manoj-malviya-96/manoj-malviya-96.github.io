Design for AM

Characterizing individual design strategies using an interactive, digital version of the Delta Design Game

Feb 14 2020

In this research, we collected eye-tracking data from nine engineering graduate students as they redesigned a traditionally manufactured part for Additive Manufacturing. Final artifacts were assessed for manufacturability and quality of final design, and design behaviors were captured via the eye-tracking data. Statistical analysis of design behavior duration shows that participants with more than three years of industry experience spend significantly less time removing material and revising than those with less experience. Hidden Markov Modeling (HMM) analysis of the design behaviors gives insight to the transitions between behaviors through which designers proceed. Findings show that high performing designers proceeded through four behavioral states, smoothly transitioning between states. In contrast, low-performing designers roughly transitioned between states, with moderate transition probabilities back and forth between multiple states.​

Data Collection

​We recruited participants with a design for additive manufacturing background. The challenge given to the participants was to re-design an airplane bearing bracket from ALCOA Corporation for AM, based on the prompt developed as part of an open crowdsource competition by GrabCAD. The design prompt asked participants to minimize mass and optimize for weight and strength while satisfying loading constraints and metal L-PBF manufacturing considerations.. Data collection was conducted in person on the research team’s data computers equipped with SolidWorks, Autodesk Fusion 360, and eye-tracking data collection capabilities.

What design strategies do intermediate-level designers employ when challenged with a novel complex problem?

Methods

​Video activities including of eye-tracking data for all participants were analyzed using the constant comparative method to develop a codebook for observational analysis using a combination of a priori and emergent coding methods. We describe “direct” design behaviors as those that directly affect the design (e.g., sketching or editing the artifact) and “indirect” design behaviors as those that represent the environment of the designer (e.g., conducting stress analysis). The design efficacy of each artifact was evaluated based on the final CAD files to quantify design quality based on the primary criteria of weight, build time estimate, support volume, total volume, and strength indicated with factor of safety. Then the participants were categorized into low-performing and high-performing designers, based on design efficacy score. Last, to understand the dynamics of designers’ behaviors, we employed Hidden Markov Modeling (HMM), a machine learning approach that elicits underlying relationships between patterns of occurrences that are not easily observable. HMM can be used to describe stochastic processes, in which a system transitions states that cannot be directly observed.

Research Findings

  • High-performing designers spend more time on indirect design activities like stress analysis, while low-performing designers focus more on removing material.
  • Design behavior was analyzed using aggregated and disaggregated Hidden Markov Models (HMMs).
  • Both models suggest two states for direct tasks (e.g., sketching, editing) and two for indirect tasks (e.g., project scoping).
  • Transmission matrices indicate designers follow an iterative design process, aligning with previous literature.
  • Low-performing designers have higher and more frequent transition probabilities, showing rapid shifts between activities.
  • High-performing designers exhibit better self-regulation, maintaining focus on tasks, unlike low-performing designers.
