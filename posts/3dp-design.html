Design for AM

Characterizing individual design strategies using an interactive, digital version of the Delta Design Game
Object Oriented Programming
Matlab
UI/UX development
Machine Learning

In this research, we collected eye-tracking data from nine engineering graduate students as they redesigned a traditionally manufactured part for Additive Manufacturing. Final artifacts were assessed for manufacturability and quality of final design, and design behaviors were captured via the eye-tracking data. Statistical analysis of design behavior duration shows that participants with more than three years of industry experience spend significantly less time removing material and revising than those with less experience. Hidden Markov Modeling (HMM) analysis of the design behaviors gives insight to the transitions between behaviors through which designers proceed. Findings show that high performing designers proceeded through four behavioral states, smoothly transitioning between states. In contrast, low-performing designers roughly transitioned between states, with moderate transition probabilities back and forth between multiple states.​

Results

Results show that high-performing designers spend much more time in indirect design activities, such as stress analysis, than the low-performing designers, who tend to focus on removing material. The design behavior heuristics were characterized from aggregated and disaggregated HMMs. Although the transmission patterns differ between the aggregate and performance-disaggregated models, each model does typically suggest that two states deal with direct design tasks such as sketching, editing, or removing material, and two that pertain to indirect design tasks such as project scoping. The transmission matrices show that designers proceed through an iterative design process, aligning with most previous design literature. However, the calculated transition probabilities show that low-performing designers have more and higher transition probabilities than high-performing designers, indicating a more rapid transition between activities. This finding indicates that perhaps high-performing designers may be better at self-regulation during the design process, staying focused on a given task, whereas low-performing designers tend not to focus on the same behaviors within the hidden states as well as the high-performing designers..

What design strategies do intermediate-level designers employ when challenged with a novel complex problem?

Details

Delta-Design is a fictional design game developed by Bucciarelli, to teach students the social designing skills such as communications, trade-offs, and team-dynamics. This game consists of a well-defined configuration design problem, but on a skewed physics system. Due to this anomaly in world, this makes it challenging for designers to apply existing domain knowledge and design heuristics. However, the objectives in the design problem are identical to typical configuration design problems, therefore, making it a research tool suitable for studying designing for structurally similar novel problem.

To facilitate design and data collection, we developed a graphical user interface (GUI) using MATLAB’s App designer. Besides facilitating the virtual design, we also focused on providing a more authentic design environment by embedding client feedback as a feature in the tool. The back end of the GUI tests the design score and satisfaction of constraints in real-time. The GUI also does not allow for impossible design features (for example, overlapping delta triangles). There are nine design actions that can be performed to complete the challenge. We place these actions on three different panels based on their roles. There are three message boxes in GUI: Delta World Status, Design Feedback, and Participant Information. The “Delta Status” message box displays the success or an error message corresponding to a designer’s actions. The “Participant Information” box displays the participant number, time left for the study and number of deltas. The “Design Score” message box is an essential feature for making this GUI a real-life experience, providing real-time insight as to the constraints when designs do not meet requirements. We also designed a control panel to provide a way to mitigate accidental mistakes. For example, if a designer accidentally deletes a triangle, they can use the UNDO function to return to the previous state. We added a panel at bottom for an overview of design constraints and objective function details. More information about the software can be found here.

Conclusion

We characterize patterns of design behaviors enacted by designers using Hidden Markov Models to investigate high- and low-performing designers (as classified using their design performance), and efficient- and inefficient designers (as classified using number of actions they took).

HMM captured variances and similarities in the patterns of behaviors and the way that different groups of designers proceeded through latent states. This data were analyzed statistically in aggregate and then by training four Hidden Markov Models to depict the underlying latent “states” of behaviors in which the designers engaged. The participants for this study were intermediate-level designers currently enrolled in graduate engineering programs. Our findings indicated that both efficient and inefficient designers could achieve high-performing client scores, but the underlying patterns of behaviors between the groups were dissimilar. Interpreted through Schraagen’s design theory, we observed:

  1. The high-performing and efficient designers indicated more methodical Progressive Deepening strategies as they systematically divided the task into small subtasks, approaching the structural components first and then optimizing the parametric components of the design later..
  2. The inefficient designers took a more trial-and-error approach throughout the process, resulting in rougher state transitions via the HMM.
  3. Results from this study show how intermediate level designers may still be developing learning strategies to apply which solving a novel design problem and illuminate how design theory manifests in a population group that is neither novice nor expert.

Further, this study is the first to demonstrate a digital version of the classic Delta Design challenge for an individual, which we posit can be used to continue to investigate the problem-solving strategies inherent in engineering design thinking.
