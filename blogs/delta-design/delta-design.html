This project dives into how engineering students approach design challenges in a digital game that tracks every step. Analyzing behaviors from 15 participants, the study reveals that high-performing designers tend to break down complex problems into manageable sub-problems and relate them to familiar concepts. Both efficient and less efficient strategies can lead to high scores, but the top performers use a progressive deepening approach—starting with foundational structure before moving into detailed optimization.

Introduction

In engineering design, problem-solving blends strategy and knowledge transfer, especially across three key approaches: control, heuristic, and learning. Research shows experts often lean on general control and heuristic strategies they’ve honed in their field, plus organized, abstract knowledge to tackle new problems. Schraagen describes two main strategies: progressive deepening—repeating sub-problems to build automatic responses—and mental simulation, where experts mentally predict outcomes before acting. Building on this, we study how graduate students solve novel problems to inform design education and computational design, aiming to support both engineering students and AI development.

This work introduces a digital Delta Design Game to study how designers tackle novel problems. The tool records each decision, allowing us to analyze behaviors of high vs. low performers and efficient vs. inefficient designers, focusing on how automaticity affects design outcomes. To this end, the overarching question this research seeks to answer is as follows:

What design strategies do intermediate-level designers employ when challenged with a novel complex problem?

Details

Delta-Design, created by Bucciarelli, is a fictional game crafted to teach students essential social design skills like communication, trade-offs, and team dynamics. The game presents a unique challenge by placing a typical configuration design problem in a world with altered physics, making it tough to apply familiar knowledge and strategies. Despite this twist, its objectives mirror real-world design tasks, making Delta-Design an ideal tool for studying how designers approach structurally similar but novel problems.

To support design and data collection, we built a MATLAB-based GUI that mimics a real-world design environment by incorporating client feedback and real-time constraint checks. The interface guides users with error and success messages and blocks impossible actions, like overlapping triangles. It includes three message boxes for feedback on Delta World Status, design details, and participant info, plus a control panel for actions like UNDO to correct mistakes. Finally, a summary panel at the bottom displays design constraints and objectives, enhancing user experience and accuracy.

Conclusion

We characterize patterns of design behaviors enacted by designers using Hidden Markov Models to investigate high- and low-performing designers (as classified using their design performance), and efficient- and inefficient designers (as classified using number of actions they took).

In this study, HMM highlighted variances and similarities in how different designer groups moved through latent states. The data was analyzed both in aggregate and through four distinct HMMs that modeled the core latent behavior “states” designers engaged in. Participants were intermediate-level designers from graduate engineering programs. Findings showed that while both efficient and inefficient designers could score well, their behavior patterns differed significantly.

Interpreted through Schraagen’s design theory, we observed:

  1. The high-performing and efficient designers used more methodical Progressive Deepening strategies by systematically dividing the task into small subtasks, first focusing on structural components and then optimizing parametric components.
  2. The inefficient designers took a more trial-and-error approach, resulting in rougher state transitions via the HMM.
  3. Results show that intermediate designers may still be developing strategies for novel problems, highlighting how design theory appears in a group that is neither novice nor expert.

Further, this study is the first to demonstrate a digital version of the classic Delta Design challenge for an individual, which we posit can be used to continue to investigate the problem-solving strategies inherent in engineering design thinking.
